HG Volatility Profile: ATR, Average Swing Size, and Risk Control

Copper futures (HG) can move slowly for hours and then launch into violent 80–150 tick runs with zero warning. If you don’t understand HG’s volatility profile — ATR, average swing size, and session volatility windows — you’re taking blind risk.

Why Understanding HG Volatility Actually Matters

HG volatility isn’t random. It’s driven by industrial demand cycles, inventory changes, and macro catalysts. Traders who size without considering volatility usually get shredded during impulsive bursts.

  • High ATR → wider stops, smaller size
  • Low ATR → tighter stops, structure-based trades
  • Volatility spikes → avoid countertrend trades

This ties directly into session behavior from Article #4.

ATR: The Backbone of HG Volatility

Average True Range (ATR) tells you exactly how much copper is moving each day. When ATR rises, HG becomes a different market — faster, harder, and more expensive.

Typical Copper ATR Ranges

  • Low ATR (0.020–0.030): Slow grind, ideal for breakout buildup
  • Medium ATR (0.035–0.050): Normal copper environment
  • High ATR (0.060+): Wild volatility, inventory shocks, macro stress

One ATR point equals $250 per contract. Respect it.

Average Swing Size in HG Futures

HG swing sizes cleanly reflect the strength of the underlying macro cycle. Swings expand when demand rises or inventories tighten. They shrink during stable or weak conditions.

Typical Swing Sizes

  • Small swings: 8–20 ticks ($100–$250)
  • Medium swings: 25–45 ticks ($312–$562)
  • Large swings: 50–80 ticks ($625–$1,000)
  • Breakout legs: 90–150 ticks ($1,125–$1,875)

These swings create the trend structure that copper is known for.

Volatility by Session: When HG Actually Moves

Session timing controls intraday volatility. This is why the contract feels dead one minute and explosive the next.

Asia Session

  • Low volatility
  • Predictable drift
  • Weak impulsive moves

European Session

  • Volatility builds
  • Directional bias forms
  • Inventory rumors hit tape

U.S. Session

  • Highest volatility of the day
  • Macro catalysts drop (ISM, PMI, jobs)
  • Trend legs expand fast

This volatility pattern amplifies when USD trends align, discussed in Article #9.

Risk Control: Sizing and Stop Placement for HG

Risk control in copper is non-negotiable. You must size positions based on volatility, not your ego or the decimals on the chart.

  • Never size a trade without checking ATR
  • Use structure stops, not arbitrary tick amounts
  • Expect slippage on high-impact data
  • Cut size when ATR spikes — copper gets dangerous

HG is one of the few metals where bad sizing is more lethal than bad entries.

Final Takeaways

Copper’s volatility profile is predictable: ATR expansions, session timing, and swing sizes tell you when the market is safe and when it’s a minefield. Respect volatility, size correctly, and HG becomes a clean, structured contract. Ignore volatility and the market will punish you every time.
