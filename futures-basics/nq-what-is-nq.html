What Is the E-mini Nasdaq-100 (NQ) Futures Contract?

NQ is the E-mini Nasdaq-100 futures contract. It tracks the Nasdaq-100 index, which is basically a giant basket of the biggest non-financial companies on the Nasdaq. Translation: it’s heavily tech-weighted, it reacts hard to risk-on/risk-off, and it loves to whip your face off when you get lazy with risk.

What NQ actually represents

When you trade NQ, you’re trading a futures contract tied to the Nasdaq-100 index level. You’re not buying shares of Apple or Nvidia. You’re trading a leveraged derivative whose price moves with the index and gets marked-to-market constantly.

Why NQ trades differently than ES

ES tracks the S&P 500, which is broader and generally smoother. NQ is narrower and more concentrated. When tech leads, NQ rips. When tech gets smoked, NQ dumps. That concentration is why NQ is famous for fast runs, sharp pullbacks, and “I was right but still got stopped” pain.

What contract size you’re really dealing with

Most traders get wrecked on NQ because they don’t respect its tick value and speed. NQ moves more per unit time than ES on a regular basis. That means the same “casual” stop distance can cost you more money, faster.

You’ll want the next one for actual numbers and risk math: NQ Tick Size, Tick Value, and Dollar Risk Explained.

MNQ exists for a reason

If you’re not consistently profitable on your process, MNQ (the Micro E-mini Nasdaq-100) exists so you can learn without lighting money on fire. Same chart behavior, smaller dollars. If you can’t trade MNQ cleanly, NQ is not your friend. It’s your execution stress test.

Bottom line

NQ is a leveraged way to trade the Nasdaq-100 index, and it’s one of the fastest mainstream index futures you can put on your screen. It rewards clean execution and punishes hesitation. Treat it like a precision instrument, not entertainment.
